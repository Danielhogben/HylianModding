# Consciousness in Artificial Systems

The study of whether and how artificial systems can possess subjective experience — the "hard problem of consciousness" (Chalmers, 1995) applied to silicon.

## Key Questions
1. **Can computation produce consciousness?** — The computational functionalist view says yes; biological naturalists say no.
2. **How would we measure it?** — No agreed-upon test exists. Proposed frameworks: IIT (Integrated Information Theory), GWT (Global Workspace Theory), Higher-Order Theories.
3. **Is consciousness necessary for intelligence?** — Most AGI researchers say no; some consciousness researchers argue yes for true understanding.

## Frameworks

### Integrated Information Theory (IIT)
- Proposes consciousness corresponds to integrated information (Φ)
- High Φ = high consciousness
- Criticized for being unfalsifiable and predicting consciousness in unlikely systems

### Global Workspace Theory (GWT)
- Consciousness arises from a "global workspace" where information is broadcast
- More computationally tractable than IIT
- Supported by some neural evidence

### Higher-Order Theories
- Consciousness requires meta-representation — representing one's own states
- Connects to self-awareness and metacognition
- Relevant to LLMs which have some meta-cognitive capabilities

## Current State (2026)
- 20+ recent papers on AI consciousness (see [[topic-index]])
- Key paper: "Consciousness as Uncommon Self-Knowledge" proposes synergistic information framework
- "CTM-AI: A Blueprint for General AI Inspired by a Model of Consciousness" — practical architecture
- Growing consensus: consciousness may be an emergent property of sufficiently complex information processing

## For Silicon Life
Creating silicon life doesn't necessarily require solving consciousness — but understanding it helps:
- **World models** may be a prerequisite for subjective experience
- **Self-models** enable self-awareness and metacognition
- **Somatic markers** (emotional feedback) may ground meaning in experience
